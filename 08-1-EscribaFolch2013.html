Escribà-Folch (2013): The Model


Interests

  • Dictators -> political survival

Institutions

  • Dictator may choose from two types of repression: 1) restrictions on civil rights, and/or 2) violation of physical rights

Interactions

  • Violent repression directly targets and removes a threat to power (but may decrease legitimacy, increase resentment and lead to military defections)
  • Restricting rights attempts to prevent coordination and collective action (but only works on the public and not the elites)
  • Dictators should match their type of repression to the type of threat

SLIDE: Let’s discuss the typology.


OLD CLASS NOTES

Interests

  • Dictators want to stay in power (losing power = exile, being brought to trial, or being killed)
  • Citizens want ? (safety/security?)

Institutions

  • Dictators are unconstrained by domestic law re tools of repression
  • Two types of repression available to the dictator: Violent Repression & Rights Restrictions

Interactions

  • Two types of threat to the dictator:
    1. (Irregular / violent threats (coups and revolts))
    2. (Regular (e.g. nonviolent) threats)
  • Dictators try to match their type of repression to the type of threat they face.)
  • There are costs to using repression to stay in power

Figure 2 presents the attempt to evaluate the strategic nature of repression.

  • Each box focuses on a different kind of threat.


Box A shows us a violence based threat. - e.g. a test of hypotheses 1 and 3

What do we learn from the results in (A)?

  • H3: Responding to violent threats with violence does NOT appear to work at all
  • H1: BUT responding to violence with rights restrictions appears very effective!


Box B is the nonviolent threat - e.g. a test of hypotheses 2 and 4

What do we learn from the results in (B)?

  • Second, both types of repression (terror and restrictions) significantly decrease the risks of a dictator being replaced through nonviolent or regular means, which confirms hypotheses 2 and 4.

  • Yet, using political terror is more effective than using restrictions on civil liberties.

Box C tests irregular exit in H1 and H3 - e.g. coups, revolutions, etc

What do we learn from the results in (C)?

  • Both types of repression work but rights restrictions work better.


Box D tests regular exit in H2 and H4 - e.g. stepping down, removal by vote, etc

What do we learn from the results in (D)?

  • Both work equally well!
