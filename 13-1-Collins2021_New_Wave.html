After 9/11, terrorism researcher David Rapoport wrote a hugely cited book, Modern Waves of Terrorism, that proposed a framework for helping us understand and analyze terrorism across time

  • In essence, he identified “distinct patterns” of terrorism across time that seemed to crash into the world like waves

  • During each wave a particular confluence of political and ideological pressures combined to produce terror groups that could be organized in certain themes

  • Anarchists, Nationalists, New Left Extremists and then Religious Extremism

  • Since that time many researchers have sought to extend the wave theory up to today and the most commonly asserted new wave focuses on right-wing terror.


This week I want us to explore this possible new wave

  • Is it sufficiently distinct to describe as a “wave”?

  • What do the groups involved in this “wave” look like?

  • And finally, are the kinds of violence pursued by these groups distinct from others?

As an alternative, and before we dive into Collins analyses, I want to show you how other researchers have sought to identify and describe possible fifth waves of terrorism

  • In writings beginning around 2007 Jeffrey Kaplan argued that the fifth wave was forming and it was predominantly ethnic in nature

  • He identified what he called a “New Tribalism” spreading through Africa primarily

  • SLIDE: In 2014 he and Christopher Costa proposed these as the checklist of the characteristics of fifth-wave ethnoterrorism


Notes from Rapoport’s writings and others

  • a generational model that linked contemporaneous global terrorist groups based on their shared characteristics of ideology/theology, strategy/tactics, and visions for the future

  • “The Four Waves of Modern Terrorism” defines a wave as a cycle that takes place over a 40-year time period. Each cycle is dynamic in that it expands and contracts.

  • Groups within a particular wave adapt to the exigencies of their time, are aware of the actions of other contemporary terrorist groups in other countries, and are driven by a relentless energy that propels them into violent action. They share a common technological facility, including communications, weaponry, and logistics. Most important, they share a vision that perceives in the ills and abuses of the world the seeds of a revolutionary future.
